## 1. Bloom's Taxonomy — Cognitive Level Alignment

Use this table to match SWBAT verbs to appropriate activity and assessment types.

### Level → Verb → Activity Matrix

**Remember** (lowest cognitive demand)
- Verbs: define, list, recall, identify, name, recognize, match
- Activity types: flashcards, matching exercises, labeling, fill-in-the-blank, recall quizzes
- Assessment: Can the student retrieve the information? Binary right/wrong.

**Understand**
- Verbs: explain, summarize, paraphrase, classify, describe, compare (basic), interpret
- Activity types: think-pair-share, concept mapping, summarization tasks, categorization, retelling
- Assessment: Can the student restate it in their own words or sort it correctly?

**Apply**
- Verbs: use, demonstrate, solve, implement, execute, construct (basic)
- Activity types: practice problems, simulations, role-play, guided exercises, structured application
- Assessment: Can the student use the knowledge in a familiar context?

**Analyze**
- Verbs: compare, contrast, differentiate, examine, organize, deconstruct, distinguish
- Activity types: case studies, debates, data analysis, compare-contrast tasks, error analysis
- Assessment: Can the student break it down and explain the relationships between parts?

**Evaluate**
- Verbs: judge, critique, justify, assess, defend, prioritize, argue
- Activity types: peer review, rubric-based assessment, pros/cons analysis, position papers, Socratic seminar
- Assessment: Can the student make and defend a judgment using criteria?

**Create** (highest cognitive demand)
- Verbs: design, construct, produce, develop, compose, generate, plan
- Activity types: projects, original compositions, design challenges, presentations, proposals
- Assessment: Can the student produce something new that meets specified criteria?

### Scaffolding Rule

SWBATs at higher Bloom's levels REQUIRE instruction that builds through lower levels first. The progression must be visible in the lesson sequence.

- If the SWBAT is at Analyze: instruction must pass through Understand and Apply before reaching Analyze tasks
- If the SWBAT is at Create: instruction must build through Understand → Apply → Analyze before creation
- Never assign a Create task when instruction only reached Remember/Understand

## 2. Gradual Release of Responsibility — Phase Details

### I Do (Direct Instruction) — 10-15% of block time

**Teacher role:** Model explicitly. Think aloud. Show the process, not just the product.
**Student role:** Observe, listen, take targeted notes.
**What to specify in the lesson plan:**
- The exact example the teacher will use
- Key language / think-aloud script (not verbatim, but the essential moves)
- What the teacher draws attention to and why
- Common misconceptions to preemptively address

### We Do (Guided Practice) — 25-35% of block time

**Teacher role:** Walk through examples WITH students. Ask guiding questions. Provide real-time corrective feedback.
**Student role:** Attempt alongside the teacher, respond to questions, try with support.
**What to specify in the lesson plan:**
- The example or problem
- The questions the teacher will ask at each step
- Expected correct responses AND common errors
- Scaffolds in place (sentence frames, graphic organizers, word banks)
- Decision point: "If students can do this with minimal prompting, move to You Do Together. If not, do another guided example."

### You Do Together (Collaborative Practice) — 25-35% of block time

**Teacher role:** Monitor, circulate, intervene only when groups are stuck. Note patterns for whole-class address.
**Student role:** Practice in pairs or small groups. Discuss, attempt, self-correct.
**What to specify in the lesson plan:**
- The task (specific prompt, not just "practice")
- Grouping strategy (random? strategic pairs? mixed ability?)
- Deliverable (what do groups produce?)
- Time limit
- What the teacher monitors for (specific look-fors)
- Intervention triggers ("If a group cannot identify the main clause, prompt with...")

### You Do Alone (Independent Practice) — 20-30% of block time

**Teacher role:** Observe, assess, note who needs follow-up. Do not re-teach to the whole class here.
**Student role:** Work independently. Demonstrate individual mastery.
**What to specify in the lesson plan:**
- The task
- Success criteria (what does a correct response include?)
- What "done" looks like
- Extension for early finishers (must deepen, not just add volume)

## 3. Formative Assessment Techniques

### Quick CFU Techniques (1-3 minutes)

**Thumbs Up/Down/Sideways** — Pulse check. Fast but low-resolution. Use for "did you get the gist?" not "can you apply this?"

**Whiteboards / Show Me** — Students write answers simultaneously and hold up. Teacher scans the room. High-resolution for factual and short-answer checks.

**Cold Call** — Teacher calls on a randomly selected student. Builds accountability. Pair with think time (3-5 seconds minimum after asking before calling).

**Turn and Talk** — 30-60 second paired discussion. Teacher listens to 2-3 pairs to sample understanding. Low-stakes, high-participation.

### Deeper CFU Techniques (3-10 minutes)

**Think-Pair-Share** — Individual reflection (1-2 min) → partner discussion (2-3 min) → whole-class share (2-3 min). The "think" phase is critical — without it, dominant students talk and others listen.

**Exit Ticket** — 1-3 written questions at end of lesson, directly tied to SWBATs. Collect and review. This is your highest-signal formative assessment.

**Gallery Walk** — Students post work, rotate to review and comment on others'. Good for peer feedback and seeing multiple approaches.

**Four Corners** — Students physically move to a corner based on their answer/opinion. Makes thinking visible and creates energy.

### Decision Rules for CFU

- CFU every 10-15 minutes of instruction, minimum
- The CFU must check the SWBAT, not just engagement or participation
- If more than ~25% of students miss a CFU: STOP. Reteach using a different explanation, example, or modality before proceeding.
- Alternate between individual CFU (whiteboards, exit tickets) and group CFU (turn-and-talk, four corners) to get both accurate individual reads and social processing

## 4. Scaffolding Strategies

Use these to support learners in reaching objectives they can't yet hit independently. All scaffolds should be designed to be removed — the goal is independence.

**Chunking** — Break complex content into smaller pieces. Teach piece 1, check, teach piece 2, check, then combine.

**Modeling with think-aloud** — Show the process AND narrate your decision-making. "I'm choosing this word because..." not just "Here's the answer."

**Graphic organizers** — T-charts, Venn diagrams, flowcharts, cause-effect maps. Provide the structure; students fill in the content.

**Sentence starters / frames** — Language scaffolds for academic discourse. "One difference between X and Y is _____ because _____." Essential for language learners but useful for all.

**Checklists** — Step-by-step procedural guides. "First do ___, then check ___, then ___." Students follow and self-monitor.

**Anchor charts** — Reference materials posted visibly. Vocabulary lists, process steps, examples. Available during practice, removed for assessment.

**Vocabulary front-loading** — Pre-teach 3-5 key terms before content instruction. Use visuals and examples, not just definitions.

## 5. Active Learning and Attention Span Rules

### The 10/2 Rule

For every 10 minutes of teacher input, provide at least 2 minutes of student processing time (writing, discussing, applying). This is non-negotiable, not aspirational.

### Attention Span Constraints

| Duration | Maximum format |
|----------|---------------|
| 0-8 min | Direct instruction / lecture (this is the hard ceiling per chunk) |
| 8-12 min | Guided practice or structured discussion |
| 12-20 min | Collaborative or independent practice |
| 20+ min | Must include a transition, movement, or format change |

### Engagement Principles

- **Movement** every 20-30 minutes. Stand up, move to a partner, gallery walk, four corners. Sitting still for 90 minutes is a design failure.
- **Variety** — alternate between individual, pair, and group work within a single session. No single format for the entire class.
- **Choice** — where possible, give students agency in how they demonstrate learning (pick your prompt, choose your format).
- **Relevance** — connect new content to prior knowledge and real-world application. The hook isn't decoration; it's the bridge from what they know to what they'll learn.
- **Productive struggle** — allow students to grapple before providing answers. Struggle builds learning. Immediately rescuing builds dependence.

## 6. Timing and Transition Guidelines

### Session Structure Template

**Opening/Hook (5-8 minutes)**
- Engage attention, activate prior knowledge, state objectives
- This is NOT administrative time. It's instructional. The hook should create a felt need for the content.

**Core Instruction Blocks (15-25 minutes each)**
- Follow GRR internally within each block
- Each block addresses one SWBAT or one chunk of a complex SWBAT

**Transitions (2-3 minutes between blocks)**
- Use a consistent signal (timer, chime, verbal cue)
- State the transition BEFORE calling it: "In 30 seconds, you'll move from pairs back to your seats"
- Post next-step instructions BEFORE the transition, not after
- Use transitions as movement/brain break opportunities

**Closing (5-10 minutes)**
- Summary tied to objectives (not "any questions?")
- Exit ticket or closing assessment
- Preview next session

**Buffer (5-10 minutes per 90-minute session)**
- Build flex time into the plan. Plans that add up to exactly the session duration will fail.
- Place buffer after the highest-risk activity (the one most likely to run long)

### Multi-day sequencing

When building units across multiple sessions:
- Each session opens by connecting to the previous session's content (not just "last time we...")
- Each session closes by previewing the next session's content
- Bloom's levels should generally progress across sessions (Day 1: Remember/Understand → Day 3: Apply/Analyze → Day 5: Evaluate/Create)
- Skills introduced in one session should be practiced in the next before new skills are added

## 7. Differentiation Strategies

### For every major activity, specify:

**Scaffold down (struggling learners):**
- Tiered tasks: same objective, reduced complexity
- Strategic pairing: pair with a stronger peer for collaborative practice
- Simplified language: provide key content in more accessible language
- Additional modeling: extra worked examples before independent practice
- Chunked instructions: break multi-step tasks into single steps

**Scaffold up (advanced learners):**
- Tiered tasks: same objective, increased cognitive demand (move up Bloom's)
- Extension questions: "Now that you've identified the pattern, why might it break down in [novel context]?"
- Peer teaching: have advanced students explain to peers (deepens their own understanding)
- Independent investigation: self-directed inquiry on a related question

### Key rule

Extensions must DEEPEN, not just ADD. "Do 10 more problems" is not differentiation. "Now try a problem where the usual approach doesn't work and explain why" is differentiation.

## 8. Slide Design Principles

### Content rules

- One idea per slide — if there are two concepts, make two slides
- 6x6 ceiling: maximum 6 bullet points, maximum 6 words per point (aim for less)
- Visual over text — use images, diagrams, charts instead of paragraphs
- Progressive disclosure — reveal information incrementally, not all at once
- Consistent layout — same slide layout for same content types throughout

### Visual design

- High-contrast text (dark on light or light on dark)
- Limit to 1 heading font + 1 body font
- Align elements to a grid
- Consistent color coding across slide types (e.g., blue for content, green for activities, orange for assessment)
- Leave white space — crowded slides kill attention
- No clip art, no decorative images that don't serve comprehension
