# SESSION 4: "Ship It & Own It"

**Sunday, Weekend 2 | 10:00 AM – 5:45 PM**
**Theme:** Final build, professional showcase, and launching a data-literate career.

---

## Learning Targets

By the end of this session, students will be able to:

1. **Present** a complete data analysis to a real audience with confidence in English (Bloom's: Evaluate → Create)
2. **Defend** methodology and conclusions under live questioning (Bloom's: Evaluate)
3. **Assemble** a professional data portfolio demonstrating the full analysis pipeline (Bloom's: Create)
4. **Articulate** their data literacy growth from Day 1 to Day 4 (Bloom's: Evaluate — metacognition)
5. **Position** data skills professionally on LinkedIn (Bloom's: Apply — career readiness)

### 10:15–11:15 | Capstone Build Sprint (60 min)

**Format:** Teams, protected build time | **GRR Phase:** You Do Together / You Do Alone | **Bloom's:** Create

#### TEACHER DOES

> **SAY:** "This is your build hour. Protected time — no lectures, no exercises. Just you and your data. By 11:15, you need:"

**Project:**
```
✓ Dashboard: complete, 5-second test ready, "so what?" headlines
✓ Memo: 1-page, recommendation first, supporting data, next steps
✓ Presentation: 5 minutes max, rehearsed at least once
✓ Team roles clear: who presents which section
```

> **SAY:** "I'm going to rotate through every team. I'll check: analysis quality, dashboard clarity, memo strength, and English presentation delivery. If you're behind, we'll triage — I'll help you scope down to something deliverable."

- **Rotation schedule:** Spend 5–7 minutes with each team. For each team, check:
  1. **Analysis:** Is the methodology sound? Are conclusions supported by the data?
  2. **Dashboard:** Does it pass the 5-second test? Are headlines insights, not labels?
  3. **Memo:** Does the first sentence tell the reader what to do?
  4. **Presentation:** Can they deliver the key points in under 5 minutes?

- **Triage students who are behind:**

> **SAY (to struggling team):** "You're trying to show too much. Pick your ONE strongest finding. Build your dashboard around that. Write the memo about that. Present that. One strong finding beats five mediocre ones."

> **SAY (at 30 min):** "Halfway check. Every team, put your hand up if your dashboard is done." Count. "Put your hand up if your memo is done." Count. "If neither is done, come talk to me right now — we need to triage."

> **SAY (at 50 min):** "10 minutes left. Start rehearsing your presentation. Time yourselves. If you go over 5 minutes, you need to cut something."

#### STUDENTS DO

- Build/finalize their capstone deliverables as a team (60 min)
- Meet with instructor for individual feedback during rotation
- Rehearse presentation at least once
- Time themselves to ensure they're under 5 minutes

#### Pacing

| Segment | Time |
|---------|------|
| Build sprint | 50 min |
| Rehearsal time | 10 min |

#### Teacher Tip

> Scope down, not up. A team with a clean 2-chart dashboard and a tight 3-minute presentation will score better than a team with a cluttered 8-chart dashboard and a rambling 7-minute talk. Help teams cut.

#### Check for Understanding

**Individual check-ins:** Every team gets 5–7 minutes of instructor time. The feedback should be specific and actionable: "Move this chart to the top — it's your strongest." "Cut the third paragraph of your memo — it repeats paragraph one."

### 12:00–12:30 | Structured Peer Review (30 min)

**Format:** Pairs/trios, rubric-based | **GRR Phase:** You Do Together | **Bloom's:** Evaluate

#### TEACHER DOES

> **SAY:** "Before you present to real guests, you present to each other. Each team will review one other team's capstone using a rubric with 3 dimensions."

**Distribute the rubric. Project it on screen:**

```
DIMENSION 1: ANALYTICAL QUALITY (1–5)
□ Is the methodology sound?
□ Are conclusions supported by the data?
□ Is there context (benchmarks, trends)?
□ Are limitations acknowledged?
SCORE: ___ /5  FEEDBACK: ___

DIMENSION 2: VISUALIZATION QUALITY (1–5)
□ Does the dashboard pass the 5-second test?
□ Are headlines insights, not labels?
□ Are chart types appropriate?
□ Is the layout clean and hierarchical?
SCORE: ___ /5  FEEDBACK: ___

DIMENSION 3: ENGLISH NARRATIVE (1–5)
□ Does the memo lead with a recommendation?
□ Is the language clear for a non-technical reader?
□ Could an executive act on this without additional explanation?
□ Is the presentation script concise (under 5 min)?
SCORE: ___ /5  FEEDBACK: ___
```

> **SAY:** "Pair up with another team. You have 15 minutes to review their dashboard, memo, and presentation outline. Fill out the rubric. Be honest — your feedback helps them improve before the showcase."

- After reviews, teams swap.

> **SAY (after 25 min):** "Read your feedback. What's the ONE thing you need to fix before the showcase?"

#### STUDENTS DO

- Review another team's capstone using the 3-dimension rubric (15 min)
- Receive review of their own capstone (10 min)
- Identify the #1 improvement to make before the showcase
- **Good feedback:** "Your dashboard passes the 5-second test, but your memo buries the recommendation in paragraph 3. Move it to the first sentence."
- **Weak feedback:** "Everything looks great." → Push: "Score each dimension 1–5. What would make it a 5?"

#### Pacing

| Segment | Time |
|---------|------|
| Explain rubric + pair teams | 3 min |
| Team A reviews Team B | 12 min |
| Team B reviews Team A | 12 min |
| Read feedback + identify #1 fix | 3 min |

#### Teacher Tip

> The rubric forces specific feedback. Without it, students say "looks good" to be polite. With it, they have to score each dimension and justify the score. This produces actionable feedback.

### 2:30–3:10 | Before & After Documentation (40 min)

**Format:** Individual writing | **GRR Phase:** You Do Alone | **Bloom's:** Evaluate (metacognition)

#### TEACHER DOES

> **SAY:** "This is the most important writing you'll do today. Not the memo — you've already written that. This is your reflection."

> **SAY:** "Write 300 words in English. Here's the structure:"

**Project:**
```
1. WHAT DATASET did you use and WHY? (2–3 sentences)
2. WHAT QUESTIONS did you ask the data? (2–3 sentences)
3. WHAT DID THE DATA TELL YOU? (3–4 sentences — your key findings)
4. WHAT DID YOU RECOMMEND and why? (2–3 sentences)
5. WHAT TOOLS did you use? (1–2 sentences — be specific)
6. WHAT WOULD YOU DO DIFFERENTLY? (2–3 sentences)
7. COMPARE: Read your Day 1 data brief. What changed about how
   you think about data? (3–4 sentences — this is the growth story)
```

> **SAY:** "Pull up your Day 1 data brief — the 3-sentence one you wrote on Day 1. Read it. Now read your capstone memo. See the difference? THAT'S what you'll write about in section 7."

> **SAY:** "You have 30 minutes. This goes in your portfolio and — for those who want it — becomes the basis of a LinkedIn post about what you learned."

- Circulate. Read over shoulders. Push students who write vaguely:
  - "What tools specifically? Name them."
  - "What would you do differently — and why?"
  - "Your Day 1 brief said 'the data was interesting.' Your Day 4 memo says 'I recommend reallocating 60% of the budget to digital channels.' What changed?"

> **SAY (at 30 min):** "Finish up. This reflection is yours — it goes in your portfolio and it captures this moment."

#### STUDENTS DO

- Read their Day 1 data brief
- Write 300-word reflection following the 7-part structure
- Compare Day 1 output to Day 4 output explicitly
- Add reflection to their portfolio
- **Good reflection:** "On Day 1, I wrote 'the data shows some patterns.' I didn't name a single number. My capstone memo starts with 'I recommend increasing investment in the southern region by 20% based on a 3-quarter growth trend of 8%.' The difference is that I now know how to let data speak — with specific numbers, context, and a recommendation that someone can act on."
- **Needs coaching:** "I learned a lot." → Push: "What SPECIFICALLY did you learn? Name one skill you didn't have on Day 1."

#### Pacing

| Segment | Time |
|---------|------|
| Explain structure + read Day 1 brief | 5 min |
| Write reflection | 30 min |
| Finish + add to portfolio | 5 min |

#### Teacher Tip

> Give students time to feel this moment. The Day 1 → Day 4 comparison is emotional for many — they'll see how far they've come. Don't rush this block.

#### Check for Understanding

**Read 2–3 reflections aloud (with permission).** The class hearing a peer articulate their growth reinforces everyone's sense of accomplishment.

### 3:25–5:00 | Capstone Showcase (95 min)

**Format:** Team presentations + Q&A to real audience | **GRR Phase:** You Do Alone / You Do Together | **Bloom's:** Evaluate → Create

#### TEACHER DOES

**Before presentations — welcome guests:**

> **SAY (to guests):** "Welcome. These students started this bootcamp 4 days ago — most had never built a pivot table or written a data brief. Today they're presenting complete data analyses with dashboards, memos, and recommendations. I'd like you to ask them tough questions — the same questions you'd ask your own team. They can handle it."

> **SAY (to students):** "This is it. 5 minutes per team, plus 3 minutes of Q&A from our guests. Timer is running. When I say 'one minute left,' start wrapping up."

**Presentation format per team:**
```
5 min — Team presents:
  • Headline finding (30 sec)
  • Dashboard walkthrough (90 sec)
  • Key supporting data (90 sec)
  • Recommendation + confidence level (60 sec)
  • Limitations + next steps (30 sec)

3 min — Guest Q&A:
  • Guests ask real questions
  • Students defend methodology and conclusions

2 min — Transition (next team sets up)
```

- **Run the showcase:**
  - Call each team up
  - Start the 5-minute timer
  - Give a "1 minute left" warning
  - Cut at 5 minutes: "Thank you. Questions from our guests?"
  - Let guests ask 2–3 questions
  - Brief applause. Next team.

- **After all teams:**

> **SAY (to guests):** "Any final observations? What impressed you?"

- Let guests give feedback. This is the culmination moment — external validation from real professionals.

> **SAY (to students):** "You just presented data to real stakeholders, under live questioning, in English. Four days ago, most of you had never built a dashboard. Remember this moment."

#### STUDENTS DO

- Present capstone as a team (5 min)
- Handle live Q&A from external guests (3 min)
- Watch peers present and take notes
- **Strong presentation:** Team leads with headline finding, walks through dashboard (not reading it — explaining what it means), gives a specific recommendation with confidence level, handles tough questions with "Based on the data, [answer]. A limitation is [x], which is why I also recommend [y]."
- **Needs support:** Freezing during Q&A → "That's a great question. Based on what we found, [answer]. If we had more time, we'd look at [related data]."

#### Pacing

| Segment | Time |
|---------|------|
| Welcome guests + rules | 5 min |
| 8–9 teams × 10 min (5 present + 3 Q&A + 2 transition) | 80–90 min |
| Guest final observations | 5 min |

#### Teacher Tip

> The guest Q&A is where the bootcamp's value becomes tangible. A finance director asking "How did you calculate this forecast?" and a student answering confidently — that's the outcome. Brief guests beforehand: "Be tough but constructive. These students are early in their data journey, but they've learned fast."

#### Check for Understanding

**The showcase IS the final assessment.** Evaluate each team on:
- Did they lead with the finding (not the process)?
- Did the dashboard pass the 5-second test?
- Was the recommendation specific and actionable?
- Did they handle Q&A with confidence?
- Was the presentation under 5 minutes?

#### Differentiation

- **Struggling teams:** Allow 6 minutes instead of 5. Provide a presentation script template if they freeze.
- **Advanced teams:** Challenge them with harder questions: "What if your data is biased? How would that change your conclusion?"
- **Language support:** Allow one team member to present in L1 if the English presentation causes them to freeze — then translate the key recommendation to English. "The skill is data presentation, not perfect English."
