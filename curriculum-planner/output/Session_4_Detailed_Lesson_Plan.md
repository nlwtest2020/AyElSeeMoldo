# SESSION 4: "Ship It & Own It"

**Sunday, Weekend 2 | 10:00 AM – 5:45 PM**
**Theme:** Capstone build, portfolio assembly, showcase, and next steps.

---

## Learning Targets

By the end of this session, students will be able to:

1. **Complete** a capstone project that demonstrates integrated AI skills
2. **Assemble** and present a professional portfolio of AI-produced work
3. **Give and receive** structured feedback using an evaluation rubric
4. **Articulate** their AI learning journey and the process behind their work (metacognition)
5. **Commit** to specific next steps for continued AI skill development

---

### 10:15–11:30 | Capstone Build Sprint (75 min)

**Format:** Individual or teams, focused build time | **GRR Phase:** You Do Alone

#### TEACHER DOES

> **SAY:** "This is the longest unbroken production block in the entire bootcamp. 75 minutes of focused build time. I'm going to protect this time — no announcements, no mini-lessons, no interruptions. Just you and your project."

> **SAY:** "I'll come around to each team for 1-on-1 coaching. When I visit, I'll ask 3 questions:"
> 1. "What are you building?"
> 2. "What are you stuck on?"
> 3. "What's the one thing that would make the biggest difference right now?"

> **SAY:** "I'll give you one specific suggestion and move on. Ready? Go."

**Coaching rotation script:**

*Rotation 1 (35–40 min):* Visit each student/team once. Spend 3–5 min per visit.

Questions to ask:
- "Show me what you have so far."
- "What's working? What's not?"
- "If you had to present in 20 minutes, what would you cut?"

Common blockers and solutions:

| Blocker | Solution |
|---------|----------|
| "I don't know what to build" | "What problem do you face at work every week? Build the solution to that." |
| "My automation isn't working" | Check authentication, check filters, test with a simplified version first |
| "I'm trying to do too much" | "Scope down. A simple, complete project > an ambitious, broken one." |
| "The AI output isn't good enough" | "Iterate the prompt. What specifically is wrong? Fix that one thing." |
| "I started from scratch today" | "OK, let's pick the simplest capstone option and make it great in the time we have." |

*Rotation 2 (25–30 min):* Revisit teams that were struggling. Focus on unblocking.

> **SAY (at 70 min):** "5-minute warning! Start wrapping up your build. After the break, you'll do peer review."

#### STUDENTS DO

- Build their capstone project (75 min uninterrupted)
- Use all tools learned across the bootcamp
- Accept coaching visits from the instructor

#### Pacing

| Segment | Time |
|---------|------|
| Rotation 1: Visit each team once | 35–40 min |
| Rotation 2: Revisit struggling teams | 25–30 min |
| 5-minute warning | at 70 min mark |

#### Teacher Tip

> Students who arrived with 70%+ complete (as instructed yesterday) will use this time for polish and iteration. Students who didn't will be scrambling. Triage: help the scramblers scope down. A simple, complete project beats an ambitious, unfinished one every time.

### 11:45–12:15 | Structured Peer Review (30 min)

**Format:** Pairs, structured rubric | **GRR Phase:** You Do Together

#### TEACHER DOES

> **SAY:** "Time to get feedback. Swap projects with another team — NOT someone you worked with. You're going to review their work using a structured rubric."

**Project and distribute the rubric:**

```
CAPSTONE PEER REVIEW RUBRIC

Reviewer: _______________ | Project reviewed: _______________

1. Does the project solve a REAL problem?          [ ] 1  [ ] 2  [ ] 3  [ ] 4  [ ] 5
   1 = toy example / 5 = solves a genuine need

2. Is AI usage transparent and intentional?         [ ] 1  [ ] 2  [ ] 3  [ ] 4  [ ] 5
   1 = unclear what AI did / 5 = clear AI role at each step

3. Could someone replicate this workflow?           [ ] 1  [ ] 2  [ ] 3  [ ] 4  [ ] 5
   1 = no documentation / 5 = step-by-step clear

4. Quality of the output                           [ ] 1  [ ] 2  [ ] 3  [ ] 4  [ ] 5
   1 = rough draft / 5 = portfolio-ready

5. What is the SINGLE STRONGEST element?
   ________________________________________________

6. What is the SINGLE WEAKEST element?
   ________________________________________________

7. One SPECIFIC, ACTIONABLE improvement suggestion:
   ________________________________________________

TOTAL: ___ / 20
```

**Scoring guide:**
- **17–20:** Excellent — portfolio-ready with minor tweaks
- **13–16:** Good — solid foundation, needs polish
- **9–12:** Developing — good concept, needs more work
- **5–8:** Early stage — needs significant revision

> **SAY:** "Review your partner's project and fill out the rubric. Be honest AND kind. Honest means: tell them what's actually weak, not just 'everything is great.' Kind means: frame it as 'here's how to make it better.' You have 12 minutes to review."

> **SAY (after 12 min):** "Now sit face-to-face with your review partner. Discuss the feedback. 10 minutes."

> **SAY (after 10 min):** "Write down the top 2 changes you'll make based on the feedback. You have 3 minutes."

#### STUDENTS DO

- Review partner's project + fill rubric (12 min)
- Receive review of own project (simultaneous)
- Face-to-face discussion of feedback (10 min)
- Note top 2 changes to make (3 min)

#### Pacing

| Segment | Time |
|---------|------|
| Review partner's project + fill rubric | 12 min |
| Face-to-face discussion | 10 min |
| Note top 2 changes | 3 min |
| Transition | 5 min |

#### Check for Understanding

**Question:** "What was the most useful piece of feedback you received?"
**Expected:** Something specific and actionable — "They said my documentation was missing the prompt I used, so I need to add that."

### 2:30–3:15 | "Making Of" Documentation (45 min)

**Format:** Individual, structured writing | **GRR Phase:** You Do Alone

#### TEACHER DOES

> **SAY:** "This is what separates a portfolio from a folder of files. You're going to write 'Making Of' documentation that explains HOW you built each artifact — not just WHAT it is. This is what employers and clients actually want to see: your process, not just your output."

> **SAY:** "Choose your 3 strongest artifacts. For each one, write a full 'Making Of' using this template. For remaining artifacts, write one sentence each."

**Project the template:**

```
"MAKING OF" — [ARTIFACT NAME]

What did I build?
(1–2 sentences describing the final output)

How was AI involved?
- Which AI tools did I use?
- What were my key prompts? (include the actual prompt text)
- How many iterations did it take?

What did AI do well?
(Specific things AI handled effectively)

What did I have to fix or override?
(Where AI fell short and what I changed manually)

What would I do differently next time?
(Lessons learned for future AI use)
```

**Show a fully worked example:**

```
"MAKING OF" — Weekly Social Media Metrics Report Automation

WHAT DID I BUILD?
A Make.com automation that pulls Instagram and Facebook engagement
metrics into a Google Sheet every Monday, generates a 5-bullet
executive summary using Claude, and emails it to my manager.

HOW WAS AI INVOLVED?
- Make.com: Built the automation pipeline (trigger → data pull → AI → email)
- Claude: Wrote the executive summary. Key prompt:
  "You are a marketing analyst. Summarize this week's social media
  metrics data in exactly 5 bullet points for a busy manager.
  Focus on: biggest growth area, biggest decline, comparison to
  last week, top-performing post, and one recommended action.
  Be specific with numbers."
- Iterations: 4 prompt versions. V1 was too generic ("engagement
  increased"). V4 included specific numbers and comparisons.

WHAT DID AI DO WELL?
- Claude was excellent at turning raw numbers into readable insights
- The "one recommended action" bullet was consistently useful
- Make.com handled the data pipeline flawlessly once set up

WHAT DID I HAVE TO FIX OR OVERRIDE?
- Claude's first draft used vague language ("significant improvement")
  — I added "Be specific with numbers" to the prompt
- Make.com's Google Sheets integration had authentication issues that
  took 15 min to debug
- The AI sometimes misidentified the top post — I added a filter to
  sort by engagement before sending to Claude

WHAT WOULD I DO DIFFERENTLY?
- Start with a simpler automation (just the data pull) and add AI later
- Test the prompt on historical data before connecting it live
- Add error handling for weeks when data is missing
```

> **SAY:** "Full 'Making Of' for your 3 strongest artifacts = about 12 minutes each. Then one-sentence descriptions for everything else = 5 minutes. Final review = 5 minutes. Go."

#### STUDENTS DO

- Choose 3 strongest artifacts (3 min)
- Write full "Making Of" for artifact 1 (12 min)
- Write full "Making Of" for artifact 2 (10 min)
- Write full "Making Of" for artifact 3 (10 min)
- One-sentence descriptions for remaining artifacts (5 min)
- Final review of complete portfolio (5 min)

#### Pacing

| Segment | Time |
|---------|------|
| Choose 3 key artifacts | 3 min |
| "Making Of" artifact 1 | 12 min |
| "Making Of" artifact 2 | 10 min |
| "Making Of" artifact 3 | 10 min |

**Expected:** Something specific — "Claude's summary was too vague, so I added 'include specific numbers' to the prompt." NOT "It was fine."

### 3:30–5:00 | Capstone Showcase (90 min)

**Format:** Presentations + Q&A | **GRR Phase:** We Do

**If guests are present (5 min):**

- 8–10 teams × 8 min each (5 min presentation + 3 min Q&A) = 64–80 min
- Buffer = 10–26 min
- If running long: reduce Q&A to 1 min per team

#### Pacing

| Segment | Time |
|---------|------|
| Welcome/context for guests | 5 min |
| 8–10 presentations × 8 min | 64–80 min |
| Buffer | 5–20 min |

#### Teacher Tip

> The 5-minute mark is firm. Students will always try to go longer. Cutting presentations short feels harsh but respects everyone's time and teaches a professional skill. The visible timer makes it impersonal — "I'm not cutting you off, the timer is."

## DIFFERENTIATION NOTES (Session 4 Summary)

| Learner Type | Strategy |
|-------------|----------|
| **Struggling learners** | Offer a capstone template with sections pre-labeled. Allow a simpler scope (2 AI tools instead of 3+). Pair with an encouraging peer reviewer. During portfolio assembly, prioritize 4–5 strongest artifacts over completeness. |
| **Advanced learners** | Ask them to present their process, not just product. Challenge them to mentor a struggling peer during build sprint. Have them write a "Making Of" that could be published as a blog post. |
| **Language support** | Allow bilingual portfolio artifacts. Provide rubric in simplified language with examples. Allow presentations in their strongest language with English slides. |
