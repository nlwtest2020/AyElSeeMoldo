# Instructional Design & Teaching Skills Reference

This document defines the pedagogical principles, instructional frameworks, and design guidelines that all curriculum agents must follow when building lesson plans and presentations.

---

## 1. Backward Design (Wiggins & McTighe)

All curriculum design begins with the end in mind:

1. **Identify desired results** - Start from SWBATs. What should students know and be able to do?
2. **Determine acceptable evidence** - What assessments will prove students met the objectives? Design these BEFORE designing instruction.
3. **Plan learning experiences** - Only now design the activities, lectures, and materials that lead students to the objectives.

Every lesson plan must trace a clear line from each SWBAT to its assessment to the instruction that supports it.

---

## 2. Bloom's Taxonomy - Cognitive Levels

Align activities and assessments to the appropriate cognitive level implied by each SWBAT:

| Level | Verbs | Activity Types |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| **Remember** | define, list, recall, identify | Flashcards, matching, labeling |
| **Understand** | explain, summarize, paraphrase, classify | Think-pair-share, concept mapping, summarization |
| **Apply** | use, demonstrate, solve, implement | Practice problems, simulations, role-play |
| **Analyze** | compare, contrast, differentiate, examine | Case studies, debates, data analysis |
| **Evaluate** | judge, critique, justify, assess | Peer review, rubric-based assessment, pros/cons |
| **Create** | design, construct, produce, develop | Projects, presentations, original compositions |

### Key Rule
- SWBATs using higher-order verbs (analyze, evaluate, create) require scaffolded instruction that builds through lower levels first.
- Never jump directly to "create" without first establishing "understand" and "apply."

## 3. Gradual Release of Responsibility (GRR)

Every new concept or skill follows this progression:

1. **I Do (Direct Instruction)** - Teacher models the skill/concept explicitly. Students observe. (10-15% of time)
2. **We Do (Guided Practice)** - Teacher and students work through examples together. Teacher provides scaffolds and corrective feedback. (25-35% of time)
3. **You Do Together (Collaborative Practice)** - Students practice in pairs or small groups with teacher monitoring. (25-35% of time)
4. **You Do Alone (Independent Practice)** - Students demonstrate mastery independently. (20-30% of time)

### Key Rule
- Never skip from "I Do" directly to "You Do Alone." Students need guided and collaborative practice.
- The teacher's role shifts from "sage on the stage" to "guide on the side" as the lesson progresses.

## 4. Formative Assessment Techniques

Embed check-for-understanding (CFU) moments throughout instruction, not just at the end:

### Quick CFU Techniques (1-3 minutes)
- **Thumbs Up/Down/Sideways** - Quick pulse check on understanding
- **Whiteboards/Show Me** - Students write answers and hold up simultaneously
- **Cold Call** - Randomly select students to answer (builds accountability)
- **Turn and Talk** - 30-second paired discussion

### Deeper CFU Techniques (3-10 minutes)
- **Think-Pair-Share** - Individual reflection, partner discussion, whole-class share
- **Exit Ticket** - Short written response at end of lesson (1-3 questions tied to SWBATs)
- **Gallery Walk** - Students review and comment on posted work
- **Four Corners** - Students move to corners based on their answer/opinion

### Key Rules
- CFU every 10-15 minutes of instruction minimum
- CFU must check the SWBAT, not just participation
- If >25% of students miss a CFU, stop and reteach before moving on
- Use a mix of individual and group CFU to get accurate reads

## 5. Scaffolding Strategies

For complex tasks, provide temporary supports that are gradually removed:

- **Chunking** - Break complex content into smaller, digestible pieces
- **Modeling** - Show worked examples with explicit think-alouds
- **Graphic Organizers** - Provide visual frameworks (T-charts, Venn diagrams, flowcharts)
- **Sentence Starters/Frames** - Give language scaffolds for academic discourse
- **Checklists** - Step-by-step procedural guides students can follow
- **Anchor Charts** - Reference materials posted visibly during the lesson
- **Vocabulary Front-Loading** - Pre-teach key terms before content instruction

### Key Rule
- Scaffolds should be designed to be removed. The goal is independence, not dependence on supports.

---

## 6. Active Learning Principles

Minimize passive listening. Maximize student engagement:

- **10/2 Rule** - For every 10 minutes of input, provide 2 minutes of processing time
- **Movement** - Incorporate physical movement every 20-30 minutes
- **Variety** - Alternate between individual, pair, and group work
- **Choice** - Where possible, give students agency in how they demonstrate learning
- **Relevance** - Connect new content to students' prior knowledge and real-world applications
- **Productive Struggle** - Allow students to grapple with challenges before providing answers

### Attention Span Guidelines
| Duration | Format |
|----------|--------|
| 0-8 min | Direct instruction / lecture (max chunk) |
| 8-12 min | Guided practice or discussion |
| 12-20 min | Collaborative or independent practice |
| 20+ min | Must include a transition, movement, or format change |

---

## 7. Lesson Timing & Transitions

### Timing Rules
- **Opening/Hook**: 5-8 minutes (engage, activate prior knowledge, state objectives)
- **Core Instruction Blocks**: 15-25 minutes each (follow GRR within each block)
- **Transitions**: 2-3 minutes between blocks (explicit signals, clear directions)
- **Closing**: 5-10 minutes (summary, exit ticket, preview next lesson)
- **Buffer**: Build in 5-10 minutes of flex time per 90-minute block

### Transition Best Practices
- Use a consistent signal (timer, chime, verbal cue)
- State the transition explicitly: "In 30 seconds, you'll move from your pairs back to your seats"
- Post next-step instructions BEFORE calling the transition
- Use transitions as quick movement/brain break opportunities

## 8. Slide Design Principles

### Content Rules
- **One idea per slide** - Each slide communicates a single concept or instruction
- **6x6 Rule** - Maximum 6 bullet points, maximum 6 words per bullet (aim for less)
- **Visual > Text** - Use images, diagrams, and charts instead of paragraphs
- **Consistent Layout** - Use the same slide layout for similar content types throughout
- **Progressive Disclosure** - Reveal information incrementally, not all at once

### Slide Types (use these categories)
| Slide Type | Purpose | Design Notes |
|------------|---------|--------------|
| **Title** | Day/module name, date, welcome | Clean, branded, minimal text |
| **Objectives** | Display SWBATs | Numbered list, student-friendly language |
| **Content** | Key concepts, definitions, frameworks | Visual + brief text, one concept per slide |
| **Example** | Worked examples, demonstrations | Step-by-step with annotations |
| **Activity** | Instructions for student work | Clear numbered steps, time limit, deliverable |
| **Discussion** | Prompts for class conversation | 1-2 open-ended questions, large font |
| **CFU/Check** | Quick assessment questions | Question + answer choices or response format |
| **Summary** | Recap key takeaways | Tie back to objectives, preview next lesson |
| **Transition** | Signal a shift between topics | Brief, visual, marks the boundary |

### Visual Design
- Use high-contrast text (dark on light or light on dark)
- Limit font variations (1 heading font, 1 body font from the brand template)
- Align elements to a grid - no floating or randomly placed items
- Use consistent color coding (e.g., blue for content, green for activities, orange for assessments)
- Leave white space - crowded slides lose attention

---

## 9. Differentiation Strategies

Build in supports for diverse learners:

- **Tiered Tasks** - Same objective, different complexity levels
- **Flexible Grouping** - Strategic pairing (mixed ability for collaboration, similar ability for targeted instruction)
- **Multiple Modalities** - Present content visually, auditorily, and kinesthetically
- **Extension Activities** - Have ready for early finishers that deepen (not just add more work)
- **Simplified Language** - Provide key content in accessible language for language learners

---

## 10. Lesson Plan Structure Requirements

Every lesson plan must include these sections in order:

1. **Header**: Day/module title, date, duration, instructor name placeholder
2. **Materials & Preparation**: Everything needed, listed with preparation notes
3. **Standards/Objectives**: SWBATs listed with Bloom's level noted
4. **Assessment Plan**: How each SWBAT will be assessed (formative and summative)
5. **Lesson Sequence**: Minute-by-minute breakdown with:
   - Timestamp (e.g., "0:00-0:08")
   - Section name
   - GRR phase (I Do / We Do / You Do Together / You Do Alone)
   - Teacher actions (what the teacher says and does)
   - Student actions (what students are doing)
   - Materials/slides referenced
   - CFU checkpoint notes
6. **Differentiation Notes**: Modifications for struggling and advanced learners
7. **Closure & Assessment**: Exit ticket or closing assessment details
8. **Reflection Space**: Blank section for post-lesson teacher notes
