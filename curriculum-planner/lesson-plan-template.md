## Worked Example

This example shows the quality bar. Note the specificity of teacher actions, student actions, and assessment. This is what "classroom-ready" means.

```
LESSON PLAN

Title: Comparing Formal and Informal Email Registers
Date: TBD
Duration: 90 minutes
Level / Audience: Upper-Intermediate Adult ESL (B2), professional development focus, 12-16 students
Instructor: TBD

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

MATERIALS & PREPARATION

□ Slides 1-18 (loaded and tested on classroom projector)
□ Handout A: Email Pair Analysis — 3 pairs of emails (same content, different registers). Print one per student.
□ Handout B: Register Feature Checklist — table with formal/informal markers. Print one per student.
□ Handout C: Email Writing Task — scenario cards (6 formal, 6 informal). Cut into individual cards before class.
□ Whiteboards + markers — one per student (for CFU)
□ Timer projected on screen (use online-stopwatch.com)
□ Exit ticket slips — half-sheet with writing prompt. Print one per student.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

LEARNING OBJECTIVES (SWBATs)

1. Students will be able to identify at least 5 linguistic features that distinguish formal from informal email register when presented with example emails. (Bloom's: Analyze)
2. Students will be able to transform an informal email into formal register, maintaining the original message while adjusting greeting, closing, vocabulary, sentence structure, and tone markers. (Bloom's: Apply)
3. Students will be able to select the appropriate register for a given professional scenario and justify their choice using at least two criteria. (Bloom's: Evaluate)

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

ASSESSMENT PLAN

| SWBAT # | Formative Check | Summative Evidence |
|---------|----------------|--------------------|
| 1 | Whiteboard check at 0:25 — students write one formal and one informal feature from Email Pair 2. Teacher scans for accuracy. If >25% miss, re-model with Email Pair 2 before proceeding. | Completed Register Feature Checklist (Handout B) with minimum 5 features per column, submitted at end of We Do. |
| 2 | Teacher circulates during You Do Together (0:48-0:65), reading drafts. Look-fors: greeting change, closing change, at least 2 vocabulary upgrades, contracted forms expanded. Flag pairs who haven't changed greeting/closing by 0:55. | Exit ticket: Transform a 4-sentence informal email into formal register (independent, collected). |
| 3 | Four Corners at 0:70 — students move to corners for "Formal," "Informal," "Either," or "Depends" for a tricky scenario. 2-3 students justify their position orally. | Embedded in exit ticket: students write one sentence explaining why they chose the register they used. |

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

LESSON SEQUENCE

0:00-0:07 | Opening Hook | — | 7 min

  Teacher does: Project two emails side by side on screen (Slide 2) — same
  message (declining a meeting), one written to a close colleague, one to a
  VP. Ask: "Both emails say no to a meeting. Why do they sound so different?
  Talk to your neighbor — 60 seconds." After 60 seconds, cold call 2-3
  students for observations. Write key student observations on the board.
  Then state: "Today we're going to figure out exactly what makes an email
  sound formal or informal, and you'll practice switching between the two."
  Display objectives (Slide 3).

  Students do: Read both emails. Discuss with neighbor. Respond when called.

  Materials: Slides 2-3

  CFU: Listen to 3-4 pairs during turn-and-talk. Are they noticing surface
  features (greetings, contractions) or going deeper (sentence length, hedging)?
  This calibrates how much modeling is needed.

  ---

0:07-0:22 | Feature Identification — Direct Instruction | I Do | 15 min

  Teacher does: Distribute Handout A (Email Pair Analysis) and Handout B
  (Register Feature Checklist). Using Email Pair 1 on screen (Slide 4-5),
  model the analysis process with a think-aloud:

  "I'm going to read both emails and look for specific differences. Let me
  start with the greeting. Email A says 'Dear Mr. Tanaka' — that's formal.
  Email B says 'Hey Kenji' — informal. I'll write 'greeting formality' as
  my first feature on the checklist."

  Continue modeling 3 more features: contractions (Email B uses "can't,"
  "won't" / Email A uses "cannot," "will not"), vocabulary choice (Email A:
  "request" vs Email B: "ask"), sentence length (Email A: longer, more
  complex / Email B: shorter, fragmented).

  Show completed checklist row for Email Pair 1 (Slide 6).

  Students do: Follow along on their own Handout A and Handout B. Fill in
  the Email Pair 1 row as teacher models.

  Materials: Slides 4-6, Handouts A and B

  CFU: None yet — this is pure modeling. Students are watching the process.

  ---

0:22-0:32 | Feature Identification — Guided Practice | We Do | 10 min

  Teacher does: Direct students to Email Pair 2 on Handout A. "Now let's do
  this one together. Read both emails silently — 90 seconds." After reading
  time: "On your whiteboard, write ONE feature you notice that's different
  between the two emails. Hold up in 3... 2... 1."

  Scan whiteboards. Pick 2-3 strong examples and 1 common error to discuss.
  Walk through Email Pair 2 as a class, building the checklist row
  collaboratively. Ask guiding questions: "What about the closing — how do
  they differ?" "Look at this sentence in Email A — what's the informal
  equivalent in Email B?"

  Students do: Read silently. Write feature on whiteboard. Participate in
  class discussion. Fill in Email Pair 2 row on checklist.

  Materials: Slides 7-8, whiteboards, Handouts A and B

  CFU: WHITEBOARD CHECK. Look for: Are students identifying structural
  features (greeting, closing, sentence patterns) or only lexical ones
  (word swaps)? If most are only finding word-level differences, explicitly
  model a structural feature before moving on.

  DECISION RULE: If >25% of whiteboards show an incorrect or superficial
  feature (e.g., "Email B is shorter" without specifying what's shorter),
  do a quick re-model: annotate Email Pair 2 on the projector highlighting
  one structural feature before proceeding.

  ---

0:32-0:35 | Transition + Movement | — | 3 min

  Teacher does: "Stand up. Find a partner who is NOT your neighbor. You have
  30 seconds." Project timer. Once paired: "With your new partner, you'll
  analyze Email Pair 3. Sit anywhere together."

  Students do: Stand, find partner, relocate, settle.

  Materials: Timer on screen

  ---

0:35-0:48 | Feature Identification — Collaborative Practice | You Do Together | 13 min

  Teacher does: "With your partner, analyze Email Pair 3 on Handout A.
  Complete the checklist row together. You should find at least 5 features.
  You have 10 minutes. When you're done, circle the feature you think is
  MOST important for setting the register — the one that, if you changed
  only that feature, would shift the email the most." Project instructions
  (Slide 9) and start timer.

  Circulate. Monitor for:
  - Pairs who are stuck (prompt: "Start with the greeting. Now compare.")
  - Pairs who found fewer than 3 features by minute 5 (prompt: "Look at
    how the request is made — compare the sentence structure.")
  - Pairs who are done early (prompt: "Did you circle your most important
    feature? Be ready to defend your choice.")

  At 0:45, call time. Quick share: 3 pairs share their "most important
  feature" choice and reasoning. Note on board.

  Students do: Analyze Email Pair 3 in pairs. Complete checklist. Circle
  key feature. Share if called.

  Materials: Slide 9, timer, Handout A and B

  CFU: Circulate and read checklists. Are pairs finding features across
  categories (lexical, structural, pragmatic)? Or clustering in one category?

  ---

0:48-0:50 | Transition to Writing Task | — | 2 min

  Teacher does: "Now you've analyzed the differences — let's see if you can
  produce them. Stay with your partner." Distribute Handout C scenario cards
  — each pair gets one informal email to transform into formal. Project
  instructions (Slide 10).

  Students do: Receive scenario card. Read instructions.

  Materials: Slide 10, Handout C (pre-cut scenario cards)

  ---

0:50-0:65 | Register Transformation — Collaborative Writing | You Do Together | 15 min

  Teacher does: "Transform the informal email on your card into a formal
  version. Write it on the back of the card. You must change at least:
  greeting, closing, 3 vocabulary items, and remove all contractions. You
  have 12 minutes. Use your checklist as a reference." Project checklist
  summary (Slide 11) and start timer.

  Circulate. Monitor for:
  - Pairs who only changed greetings/closings but left informal vocabulary
    (prompt: "Read your third sentence aloud — does it sound formal?")
  - Pairs who over-formalized to the point of awkwardness (prompt: "Would
    you actually send this to a manager? Does it sound natural?")
  - Flag any pair that hasn't changed their greeting/closing by 0:55 —
    direct intervention.

  At 0:62, pairs swap cards with another pair for quick peer review
  (2 minutes): "Read their formal version. Put a star next to any sentence
  that still sounds informal."

  Students do: Transform email collaboratively. Use checklist as reference.
  Peer review another pair's work.

  Materials: Slides 11-12, timer, Handout C, Handout B (reference)

  CFU: Peer review stars serve as formative data. After swap-back, ask:
  "Raise your hand if your reviewers found something informal you missed."
  If more than half the class raises hands, do a 2-minute whole-class
  calibration on the most common miss.

  ---

0:65-0:72 | Register Selection — Evaluative Discussion | We Do → You Do Together | 7 min

  Teacher does: Project a tricky scenario (Slide 13): "You need to ask your
  direct supervisor — who you're also friends with outside work — to approve
  your vacation request. What register do you use?"

  Four Corners: point to corners labeled "Formal," "Informal," "Either,"
  "Depends." Students move. Ask 1 student from each corner to justify.
  Facilitate brief discussion.

  Then project a second scenario (Slide 14): "You're emailing a client
  you've worked with for 5 years about a project delay." Same exercise.

  Students do: Move to corners. Justify orally. Listen to other positions.

  Materials: Slides 13-14, corner labels (posted before class)

  CFU: FOUR CORNERS. Listen for justification quality — are students citing
  specific features (relationship, power dynamic, stakes, audience
  expectations) or just saying "it feels formal"? If justifications are
  vague, prompt: "What specifically about this situation makes you choose
  that register?"

  ---

0:72-0:75 | Transition to Independent Practice | — | 3 min

  Teacher does: "Return to your seats. For the last part of class, you'll
  work on your own." Distribute exit ticket slips. Project instructions
  (Slide 15).

  Students do: Return to seats. Read exit ticket prompt.

  Materials: Slide 15, exit ticket slips

  ---

0:75-0:85 | Independent Writing | You Do Alone | 10 min

  Teacher does: "On your exit ticket, you'll see a 4-sentence informal
  email. Transform it into formal register. Below your formal version,
  write one sentence explaining why formal register is appropriate for this
  scenario. You have 8 minutes. Work silently and independently. Use your
  checklist if you need it."

  Circulate silently. Note students who are struggling (for follow-up) and
  students who finish early. Do not provide help unless a student is
  completely stuck — this is assessment, not practice.

  Early finisher extension (Slide 16): "Now transform your formal version
  into a DIFFERENT informal register — imagine you're texting a colleague
  the same message. What changes? What stays?"

  Students do: Write independently. Use checklist as needed.

  Materials: Slide 15-16, exit ticket slips, Handout B (reference)

  CFU: This IS the assessment. Collect exit tickets.

  ---

0:85-0:90 | Closing | — | 5 min

  Teacher does: "Before you go — turn to a neighbor and tell them one thing
  you'll check next time you write a professional email that you wouldn't
  have checked before today." Allow 60 seconds. Then: "We'll pick this up
  next session with a focus on tone — not just formal vs. informal, but
  how to sound confident vs. tentative in professional writing."

  Collect exit tickets at the door.

  Students do: Share with neighbor. Hand in exit ticket on the way out.

  Materials: Slide 17-18

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

DIFFERENTIATION NOTES

For Email Pair 3 Analysis (collaborative practice):
  Scaffold down: Provide a partially completed checklist row with 2 features
  already filled in. Pair struggling students with a stronger partner.
  Scaffold up: After completing the checklist, have pairs rank their features
  by "impact on perceived formality" and write a 2-sentence justification for
  their #1 choice.

For Register Transformation (collaborative writing):
  Scaffold down: Provide a sentence-level frame: "Original: [informal
  sentence]. Formal version: [blank]." Students work sentence by sentence
  rather than rewriting the whole email at once.
  Scaffold up: After transforming, have pairs write a 3-sentence reflection:
  "The hardest feature to change was ___ because ___. The most important
  feature to change was ___ because ___."

For Exit Ticket (independent):
  Scaffold down: Underline the specific words/phrases in the informal email
  that need to change (visual cue to reduce cognitive load).
  Scaffold up: Early finisher extension is built into the plan (text message
  register transformation on Slide 16).

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

CLOSURE & ASSESSMENT

Exit Ticket (SWBAT 2 + SWBAT 3):
Prompt: "Transform this informal email into formal register. Then write
one sentence explaining why formal register is appropriate for this
scenario."

[Informal email printed on slip]:
"Hey Sarah — Can't make the Thursday meeting, something came up. Can we
push it to Friday? Let me know, thanks! — Jamie"

Collected at the door. Review same evening to identify students who need
follow-up on register features.
```

## Quality checklist — verify before delivering

Run through this list before handing any lesson plan to the user:

- [ ] Every SWBAT uses an observable, measurable verb (not "understand," "learn about," "appreciate")
- [ ] Every SWBAT has a Bloom's level tag
- [ ] Every SWBAT has both a formative check and summative evidence in the assessment plan
- [ ] Instruction builds through Bloom's levels (never jumps to Create from Remember)
- [ ] GRR sequence is present: I Do → We Do → You Do Together → You Do Alone (no phase skipped)
- [ ] CFU appears at least every 15 minutes
- [ ] Each CFU specifies technique, what's being checked, and a decision rule for failure
- [ ] Timeline math adds up (activities sum to ≤ session duration minus buffer)
- [ ] No single activity format exceeds 12 minutes without a shift
- [ ] Buffer time is built in (5-10 min per 90-min session)
- [ ] Transitions are explicit (signal, directions, timing)
- [ ] Differentiation is specified for each major activity (scaffold down + scaffold up)
- [ ] Materials list is complete enough for a substitute teacher
- [ ] Activity descriptions specify: prompt, grouping, deliverable, time limit, and teacher monitoring focus
- [ ] Reflection space is included
